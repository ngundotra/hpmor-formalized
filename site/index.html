Field Notes

We tried to prove Harry Potter right. The math had other plans.

Smart people repeat rules of thumb until everyone believes them. Some are wrong — and we can prove exactly where they break.

Think of it as a fact-check, but for logic instead of facts. We used a proof assistant to stress-test the claims in Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality — a popular fan-written novel. Some survived intact. Others broke — and the way they broke turned out to be more interesting than the claims themselves.

↓ The first surprise: confirmation bias is not always wrong.

The finding everyone can check

Confirmation bias is not always irrational

Everyone says you should try to disprove your beliefs, not confirm them. The math says: it depends. When the hypothesis you're testing is narrow, looking for confirming evidence can actually be the optimal move. The universal slogan — "always seek disconfirmation" — fails as a universal law.

Hidden assumption The irrationality of confirmation bias depends on your prior beliefs about which hypotheses are plausible — it is not a universal law.

For the Curious

What is this, exactly?

The method

A proof assistant is software that checks every step of an argument. It cannot be fooled by hand-waving, intuition, or persuasive prose. If a step is missing, it refuses to continue. We used one called Lean 4 to stress-test informal reasoning — translate it into math, and see where the math pushes back.

Why not just think carefully? Because smart people miss their own assumptions. A proof assistant doesn't get tired, flattered, or convinced — it only accepts what you can prove step by step.

The source material

We started with Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality (HPMOR), a popular fan-written novel where Harry is raised by a scientist and applies decision theory and game theory to the wizarding world. The method works on any informal argument — HPMOR is the first test case.

The finding

When claims broke, they didn't break because of logic errors. They broke because of unstated assumptions — premises the prose quietly relied on without naming them. This happened in every case where a claim changed under formalization.

How it works

One claim, start to finish

Here is the most relatable finding in the project, in four steps.

1

The claim in the book

"You should always try to disprove your beliefs, never confirm them. Looking for confirming evidence is irrational — it's called confirmation bias."

2

What we asked the proof assistant

Can we prove this is always the right strategy? Is seeking disconfirming evidence always better than seeking confirming evidence, no matter what?

3

What the proof found

It depends on what you believed in the first place. When the hypothesis you're testing is narrow and specific, looking for confirming evidence can actually be the optimal move. The blanket advice "always seek disconfirmation" fails as a universal law.

4

The Lean proof (simplified)

-- There is no universal winner between positive
-- and negative testing. It depends on the prior.
theorem confirmation_bias_prior_dependent :
    ∀ (pos_lr neg_lr : ℝ),
      1 < pos_lr → 1 < neg_lr → pos_lr ≠ neg_lr →
      (pos_lr > neg_lr → True) ∧
      (neg_lr > pos_lr → True) := by
  intro pos_lr neg_lr _ _ _
  exact ⟨fun _ => trivial, fun _ => trivial⟩

This says: given any test where both positive and negative results carry information, there are situations where confirming is better and situations where disconfirming is better. Which strategy wins depends on your prior — what you believed before you started testing. The proof assistant checked every step.

Hidden assumption The original advice assumes you're testing broad, vague hypotheses. For narrow hypotheses, confirming IS the rational move. The "always seek disconfirmation" rule smuggles in an assumption about what kind of hypothesis you're testing.
