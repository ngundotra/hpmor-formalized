Field Notes

What breaks when you fact-check logic instead of facts?

We tried to translate common advice about rational thinking into math that a computer could check. The computer's first question, every time, was: what are you assuming? Turns out, the assumptions were doing most of the work — and nobody had named them.

↓ For example: "be transparent" assumes everyone listening is on your side.

For the Curious

What is this, exactly?

The finding

When we tried to formalize common advice, we couldn't even write the theorem until we'd stated assumptions the original argument never mentioned. The proofs were often simple once the assumptions were named. The assumptions were the finding.

The source material

We started with Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality (HPMOR), a popular fan-written novel where Harry is raised by a scientist and tries to apply rational thinking to the wizarding world. The method works on any informal argument — HPMOR is the first test case.

The method

Think of it as a tool that won't let you skip steps. A proof assistant refuses to accept a claim until you've stated every assumption it depends on. It doesn't just check your logic — it forces you to say out loud what you were assuming in silence. We used one called Lean 4.

Why not just think carefully? Because smart people don't notice their own assumptions. A proof assistant won't let you move forward until you've named every one.

How it works

One claim, start to finish

Here is a finding everyone can relate to, in four steps.

1

The advice

"Be transparent. Honesty is the best policy."

2

We tried to formalize it

Before we could even write the theorem, the proof assistant asked a question the original advice never answers: who is listening? We had to add a parameter — how dangerous are the people who overhear you? — before the math would even run.

3

The assumption we were forced to state

"Be transparent" assumes a friendly audience. When we made that assumption explicit, we found the advice has a tipping point: when opponents can hurt you more than allies can help you, transparency backfires. The advice is only good advice when everyone listening is on your side.

4

What the computer forced us to write down

-- "For any communication strategy..."
theorem optimal_targeting

  -- We had to define allyBenefit and opponentPenalty
  -- — concepts the original advice never mentions —
  -- before Lean would let us state the theorem.

  -- "...where allies learn your secret
  --    with some probability (α, between 0 and 1)..."
  (α : between 0 and 1)

  -- "...and opponents learn it with
  --    some probability (β, between 0 and 1)..."
  (β : between 0 and 1)

  -- "...the payoff is always ≤ the payoff when
  --    allies know everything (α = 1)
  --    and opponents know nothing (β = 0)."
  : payoff(α, β) ≤ payoff(1, 0)

-- The proof is straightforward algebra.
-- The hard part was noticing that "who is listening?"
-- was a question the original advice never asked.

The proof itself is straightforward — once you've named the assumptions, the math follows quickly. The hard part was noticing that "who is listening?" was a question the original advice never asked. The full version is in the source file.

Hidden assumption The advice "be transparent" contains an unstated assumption: that everyone listening benefits from your honesty. Once you name that assumption, you can see exactly when the advice fails.
