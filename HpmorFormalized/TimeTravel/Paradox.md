In HPMOR, Time-Turners enforce a strict rule: if you try to create a paradox (like preventing your own time travel), the universe finds a self-consistent resolution, often a surprising "third option" the traveler did not anticipate. We modeled this as the fixed-point problem for a round-trip map on a finite state space: a state is "consistent" exactly when the map sends it to itself. We proved that a fixed-point-free map (the paradox) can always be resolved by extending the state space with at least one new "self-stabilizing" state, and that any such fixed point must come from the extension, not the original states. Connecting this to the Novikov self-consistency principle from the companion module, we showed that even if a single-step map has no fixed point, some iterate always does on a finite type -- paradoxes are resolved by allowing multi-step loops, with the resolution period provably at least 2. The formalization revealed a hidden assumption in the HPMOR narrative: it is not enough to simply add new states to resolve a paradox; those states must genuinely be self-stabilizing (mapped to themselves by the round-trip function). This sharpens the informal intuition that the "universe override" is not arbitrary but structurally necessary.
