In HPMOR Chapter 17, Harry argues that before testing a hypothesis, you pay an information-theoretic cost just to "locate" it among all possible hypotheses. If there are N hypotheses with a uniform prior, each gets probability 1/N, and specifying one costs log2(N) bits. As the hypothesis space grows, the prior on any single hypothesis shrinks exponentially. Harry implies this cost is intrinsic to the hypothesis's complexity, relating it to Solomonoff induction where simpler hypotheses (shorter programs) get higher prior weight. The formalization confirms the finite-space version but reveals that the "locating cost" depends on the choice of description language: two different languages can assign different description lengths to the same hypothesis. The invariance theorem bounds this difference by an additive constant, but that constant is itself language-dependent, so there is no language-independent notion of "complexity" as HPMOR suggests.
