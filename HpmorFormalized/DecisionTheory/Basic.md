In HPMOR (Chapters 33, 75-77), Harry argues that a rational agent can gain an advantage by credibly committing to a strategy before the game begins -- even if that strategy looks irrational in the moment. We modeled this as a two-player finite game and formalized the Stackelberg leader advantage: a player who commits first and lets the opponent best-respond achieves a payoff at least as high as their Nash equilibrium payoff, and strictly higher when the Nash strategy differs from the Stackelberg optimum. We then proved the "self-binding paradox": if the commitment is credible (the leader would follow through anyway), the outcome is just a Nash equilibrium and no real advantage was gained. The contrapositive is the key HPMOR insight: beneficial precommitment is necessarily incredible -- you must genuinely restrict your future freedom for the commitment to help. Formalization revealed a hidden assumption: the HPMOR argument implicitly requires that the precommitment beats the *best* Nash equilibrium payoff, not just any Nash equilibrium, since games can have multiple Nash equilibria with different payoffs for Player 1. The Prisoner's Dilemma is included as a concrete example where (Defect, Defect) is the unique Nash equilibrium and cooperation via precommitment is provably incredible -- confirming that Harry's cooperation gambit requires an irrevocable commitment device.
