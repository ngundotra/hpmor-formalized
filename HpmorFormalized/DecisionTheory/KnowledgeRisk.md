In HPMOR Ch. 119, the Interdict of Merlin restricts powerful magical knowledge on the grounds that the creation/destruction asymmetry makes open dissemination net-negative: a single bad actor with world-destroying knowledge causes more harm than many good actors cause benefit. We formalized this as a model with N agents, each independently constructive (probability p_good, value V) or destructive (probability p_bad = 1 - p_good, harm H < 0 with |H| >> V). Open dissemination yields expected utility N * (p_good * V + p_bad * H), which is provably negative when |H|/V > p_good/p_bad — confirming Harry's asymmetry argument. Under restriction with per-agent leak probability q, expected utility is exactly q times the open-dissemination utility, so any q < 1 strictly helps. The restriction threshold is trivially q < 1 — there is no subtle cutoff. The deeper finding concerns population scaling: containment probability (1-q)^N decreases exponentially in N, so restriction becomes exponentially harder to maintain as the population grows. Formalization also reveals that restriction merely scales the problem without changing its character (restricted EU = q * open EU). The Interdict of Merlin works in HPMOR because it is magically enforced (q approximately 0); for mundane information restrictions, the security-through-obscurity critique applies and containment degrades with population size. Harry's argument survives formalization in the static case, but the population scaling result shows that long-term restriction is a race against exponential leak probability growth.
